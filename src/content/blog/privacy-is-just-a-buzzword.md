---
title: 'Privacy is just a buzzword in modern software'
description:
  'Worrying too much about privacy in modern-day software will exhaust you before you ever find it'
pubDate: '2026-02-16'
tags: ['Privacy', 'Browsers', 'OPSEC']
---

Few months back, I was looking for a privacy-focused browser. Google is an advertisement company, so
it definitely collects data. And Firefox is messing around with AI and stuff and had changed the
privacy policy lately. So we cannot trust it anymore in a way that they don’t sell our user data to
some other company.

In that matter, once I heard Primeagen saying Chrome is more secure than Firefox. Because at least
we have an idea who’s collecting and using our data. Lol. Yeah, that’s a fair point. Tried out so
many browsers, and at one point, you’re going to feel like nothing works. Yeah, it’s right. If we
say so, nothing will work. And then you will understand privacy is just a selling word. It’s like
unlimited data. You know, still they can get people by using those selling words. Not just in
browsers, even when it comes to VPN and stuff, the situation is the same.

So what is the solution?

In my opinion, you cannot find any product that is completely privacy-focused. The idea of this
statement could depend on the context where you use it. There are some privacy-focused VPN providers
that we can put our trust on. But I don’t know how much we can trust them.

Back to browsers, you cannot find a fully privacy-focused browser. If you want one, go and make one
yourself. That’s the only solution. But it’s not practical for everybody, as we already know.

What I do is, I settled up with Zen, which is a fork of Firefox but with those AI things removed
from the codebase and some common telemetry disabled by default. It’s not intense about privacy like
LibreWolf. It’s practical, and we have some level of privacy there.

We can’t use TOR on a daily basis, and Mullvad has a browser which is good for specific scenarios.
But I don’t think that’s good for normal usage.

There are some Firefox-based privacy-focused browsers like LibreWolf, as I mentioned earlier. But
the issue is, sometimes they are a bit behind the latest updates of Firefox, which can be a serious
issue.

The point being, don’t focus too much on privacy in a browser. Just concern about it up to a certain
level and focus more on the requirements that you are looking for in a browser.

When it comes to my scenario, I just wanted a browser which has some process-level separation when
it comes to a profile.

Chrome doesn’t provide process-level separation. It uses the same process across all the profiles.
But Firefox-based browsers do this. They separate profiles at the process level. Each profile gets
its own process. This gives you profile-level isolation.

So that was my requirement, and with some privacy, I settled on Zen browser.

So don’t care too much about browser privacy, and it will exhaust you in the process of finding that
kind of a browser.

For investigations and security-related things, I have some other setups which I don’t combine with
my personal life stuff. Don’t be amazed. I don’t use any special kind of browsers there to prevent
being tracked. The concept being, I use some separate environments, and any data collected from
those setups, no one can correlate them to my personal identity. That’s the strategy you should use
too instead of worrying too much about privacy in a browser if you need some OPSEC.
